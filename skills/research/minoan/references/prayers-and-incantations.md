# Prayers, Spells, and Incantations — Shirat Ha Kotharot

Liturgical texts from Tom di Mino's poetry collection *Shirat Ha Kotharot* ("The Song of the Muses"), rendered in reconstructed Minoan. These are modern compositions in the ancient register—not translations of Linear A inscriptions, but original prayers inhabiting the Minoan-Semitic voice.

All Minoan renderings follow pre-begadkephat phonology: p/t/k are always stops, never f/θ/x. Hebrew forms are restored to their Bronze Age ancestors. Poetic variants (e.g. *ani* alongside the archaic *anāk*) are preserved where the original voice requires them.

---

## I. Shirat Ha Yamu — שירת הים
*(Or, "The Song of the Serpent's Sea")*

### Minoan Rendering

> Am ha Yam ani
> u bēti Kaptur
> Baruk ʾiyat Tinit
> ki neʾmar
> Kretanit yōdaʿanit
> Baʿalat barat
> ashar min-ki
> ani ʿanap
> ani sarap
> ṭabul ba-tahāmat

### Translation

> I am a Sea People
> And my home is Kaphtor
> Blessed isle of Tinit
> For it is said:
> A wise Cretan woman
> You created, Mistress
> From whom
> I am a branch
> I am a serpent-fire
> Steeped in the Deep

Mine is the ancient song
and it flows through everyone
of every age

### Phonological Notes

| Hebrew Original | Minoan Form | Rule Applied |
|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| ani | **ani** | Poetic variant; archaic form *anāk* (Ug. *ank*) also attested |
| Kaphtor | **Kaptur** | Pre-begadkephat: /p/ not /f/; no spirantization |
| Baruch | **Baruk** | √b-r-k; final /kh/ → /k/ (pre-begadkephat) |
| saraf | **sarap** | √ś-r-p; final /f/ → /p/ (pre-begadkephat) |
| tehomet | **tahāmat** | Minoan *tahām* (Ug. *thm*); fem. ending *-at* |
| anaf | **ʿanap** | √ʿ-n-p; final /f/ → /p/ (pre-begadkephat) |
| ne'emar | **neʾmar** | √ʾ-m-r; N-stem passive "it is said" |
| Jed'anit | **yōdaʿanit** | √y-d-ʿ + gentilic *-anit*; "knowing-woman" |

### Liturgical Context

An identity-prayer. The speaker claims membership in the *ʿAmm ha Yamm* — the Sea People — and traces descent from the Creatress through Kaphtor. The **sarap** (seraph/serpent, √ś-r-p "to burn") connects the speaker to chthonic power: the snake-priestess tradition of Minoan Crete. **Ṭabul ba-tahāmat** ("steeped in the Deep") invokes immersion in the primordial waters — Tahāmat, cognate with Tiamat.

## II. Shirat Ha Kotharot — שירת הכתרות
*(Upon hearing Genesis in Biblical Hebrew)*

### Minoan Rendering

> Aum, ʿayin, aum…

> Bi-rēshit Baʿalat barat
> Ilīm u ha Shamayīm
> ʾat u ʾArṣ

### Translation

> O eternal eye, O womb…

> In Beginning, Mistress you created
> The gods and the heavens
> And the essence of the Earth

In the void of grammar, we confined her
and all her Mysteries
but She is wiser

*Begin with aum, ʿayin, aum until it becomes a stream, then flow into the Bi-rēshit Herself. You will be sparked by the sounds of Rūḥ ha Qadish.*

### Phonological Notes

| Hebrew Original | Minoan Form | Rule Applied |
|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| Beresheit | **Bi-rēshit** | Prep. *bi-* + *rēshit* |
| Eilim | **Ilīm** | Ug. *ʾilm* "gods"; not the later Hebrew *Elohim* |
| Haretz | **ʾArṣ** | Ug. *arṣ*; emphatic /ṣ/ preserved |
| Ruach ha Qadesh | **Rūḥ ha Qadish** | Pre-spirantization; Ug. *rḥ* + *qdš* |

### Liturgical Context

A creation-invocation that regenders Genesis 1:1. **Baʿalat barat** — "Mistress, you created" — replaces the masculine *bara Elohim* with feminine divine agency. The mantra instruction (*aum, ʿayin, aum*) is a meditation technique: the Hebrew letter ʿayin (eye/source) becomes a breathing portal into the Goddess's creative speech.

## III. Kepitzat Ha Darik — קפיצת הדרך
*(Re: Ezekiel 25:16–17 and the Mycenaeans)*

### Minoan Rendering

> Kōtarāt ha Kanasāt, Baʿalat barat
> Ṣadiqat ha Ṣadiqāt, Baʿalat barat
> Qadeshat ha Qadishāt, Baʿalat barat
> Ṣadiqu ha Ṣadiqīm, Baʿalat barat
> (ki hinēnī, ʾish mi mayy meraḥapat)
> Ya-wa-ʾIl ha ʾIlīm, Baʿalat barat
> Pelisht ha Pelishtīm, Baʿalat barat
> Kanasāt ha Kōtarāt, Baʿalat barat

### Translation

> Muses of the Assemblies, Mistress you created
> Righteous woman of the Righteous, Mistress you created
> Holy woman of the Holy, Mistress you created
> Righteous man of the Righteous, Mistress you created
> (for here, fire from water hovers)
> Cretan god of the Gods, Mistress you created
> Philistine of the Philistines, Mistress you created
> Assemblies of the Muses, Mistress you created

> Anāk Pelishtīm
> Anāk Kanaʿanīm
> Anāk Yahūdīm

> We bleed as they bleed,
> and our souls are one

> Barukīm ʿAmm ha Yamm, Kanasat ha Kanasāt

### Phonological Notes

| Hebrew Original | Minoan Form | Rule Applied |
|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| Knossot | **Kanasāt** | √k-n-s "to gather"; Knossos = "Gathering Place" |
| Kotharot | **Kōtarāt** | √k-t-r; fem. pl. *-āt* |
| Tzadikit | **Ṣadiqat** | √ṣ-d-q; pre-begadkephat /q/ not /k/ |
| Qadeshet | **Qadeshat** | √q-d-sh; pre-begadkephat /sh/ preserved |
| Philisti | **Pelisht** | Pre-begadkephat /p/ not /f/; older form |
| Anoki | **Anāk** | Archaic 1cs (Ug. *ank*) |
| esh | **ʾish** | Ug. *ʾišt* "fire" |
| merachafet | **meraḥapat** | √r-ḥ-p; pre-begadkephat /p/ not /f/ |

### Liturgical Context

An incantation of unity. The sevenfold **Baʿalat barat** refrain hammers the feminine creative principle across every stratum of Bronze Age identity — Muse, priestess, prophet, god, Philistine. The chiastic structure (Kōtarāt ha Kanasāt → Kanasāt ha Kōtarāt) creates a ritual closure. The closing line in Hebrew script — **ברוכים עם הים, כנסת הכנסות** — declares the Sea People blessed, gathered in the Gathering of Gatherings.

## IV. Dabarīm Kapturīm — דברים כפתורים
*(Or, "Cretan Commandments" in Phoenician)*

### The Seven Precepts

**I.**
> Bi-yad puluḥ ʿidān ḥaddu — ʿal qašqash, ʿal ʾaban, ʿal qanah.

"By hand, revere the sharp moment — upon scale, upon stone, upon reed."

**II.**
> Qarāʾtu ʿamm-ī: yilbashū ʿōr-ī, hin ʾaḥzē ʾaḥarīt u qadmōnīt — ʿidanāt ḥurāṣ lipnē kull u ʾaḥarē kull.

"I called out to my people: they may wear my hide, if I may divine the future and the ancient-past — ages of gold before all and after all."

**III.**
> Qayinat-nā ashar bi-Shamē u bi-ʾArṣ, tanashshipu dabarāt-hā bi-ʾuznāt ʾamunōt.

"Our Creatrix, who is in the Heavens and the Earth, breathes forth her words through the ears of the faithful."

**IV.**
> Ani malāḥ — u ṣillu naṣar min ʿammīm, maqōmāt, dōrōt.

"I am a sailor — a shadow glimpsed from peoples, places, eras."

**V.**
> Nōśiʾ ṣōripī lahabu ʾishtu kīma mayim, nāshipu ʿabur barqu — naʿlē maʿaṭ ʿillōy, u naḥpur ʿamīq, yōman kull u kull.

"Wielding a refiner's blade of fire like water, breathing through lightning — may we rise a little higher, and dig deeper, daily, all and all."

**VI.**
> Kull ʿōlām tashbītu, ka ḥarāshat u kiništu, lā-ʿōlām dōrī.

"All eternity is a reverberation; like craft and civilization, forever cyclical."

**VII.**
> Watiqat Qadeshat ha tōr ba-qarnayim.

"The priestess takes the bull by the horns."

## V. Ras Melqart — ראש מלקרט
*(Or, "Herakleia Minoa" cf. Gaza)*

### The Closing Incantation

> anāk rishʿat-hā: malak mi mayy
> mayy min nahar, nahar min yamm

> I am her message: an angel from water
> water from river, river from sea

### Liturgical Context

A chain-incantation tracing sacred water backward from the individual to the cosmic. **Malak mi mayy** — "messenger/angel from water" — plays on the double meaning of √m-l-k (king/messenger). The chain (water → river → sea) reverses the hydrological cycle into a genealogy of divine origin: all life descends from Yamm.

## VI. Baʿalat ʿAkbarat — בעלת עכברת
*(Or, "Laylat Al-Qadrat" in retort to Sheikh Jamil's philology)*

> Al-Lat, Al-ʿUzzat, Al-Manāt
> ʾAhiyat ʾAshirat ʾAhiyat
> Rabbat Hawwat Eilat

### Liturgical Context

A triple-goddess triptych. Three divine triads stacked:

1. **Al-Lat, Al-ʿUzza, Al-Manat** — the Arabian triple goddess (pre-Islamic, Nabataean)
2. **ʾAhiyat ʾAshirat ʾAhiyat** — "I am Asherah, I am" — mirrors Exodus 3:14 (*ʾEhyeh ʾAsher ʾEhyeh*) regendered. The pun on *ʾAsher* / *ʾAshirat* collapses the relative pronoun into the divine name.
3. **Rabbat Hawwat Eilat** — "Great Lady, Life-Giver, Goddess" — three Canaanite epithets (√r-b-b, √ḥ-w-y, √ʾ-l)

## Composition Notes

### Pre-Begadkephat Restoration Table

Every Hebrew word in these poems has been checked against pre-begadkephat phonology. The six begadkephat consonants (בגדכפת) revert to their stop values:

| Letter | Hebrew (post-) | Minoan (pre-) | Example |
|--------|---------------|---------------|---------|
| ב | /v/ after vowel | always /b/ | *ʾavad* → **ʿabad** |
| ג | /ɣ/ after vowel | always /g/ | *nagid* → **nagid** |
| ד | /ð/ after vowel | always /d/ | *yodēaʿ* → **yōdaʿ** |
| כ | /x/ after vowel | always /k/ | *barukh* → **baruk** |
| פ | /f/ after vowel | always /p/ | *saraf* → **sarap** |
| ת | /θ/ after vowel | always /t/ | *ʾemet* → **ʾamat** |

### Pronoun Variants

| Form | Register | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| **anāk** | Archaic/formal | Ug. *ank*, Phoen. *ʾnk*; older 1cs |
| **ani** | Poetic/intimate | Later form; acceptable in lyric register |
| **ʾatta** | — | 2ms |
| **hiya** | — | 3fs |
