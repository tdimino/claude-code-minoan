# Minoan Phonology — Phoenician Priority

The sound system of reconstructed Minoan, calibrated to the earliest recoverable Northwest Semitic stage. Pronunciation follows Phoenician-Ugaritic norms, not later Hebrew.

## Consonant Inventory

### Stops (Pre-Begadkephat)

| Place | Voiceless | Voiced | Emphatic | Linear A |
|-------|-----------|--------|----------|----------|
| Labial | **p** /p/ | **b** /b/ | — | PA-PU (merged) |
| Dental | **t** /t/ | **d** /d/ | **ṭ** /tˤ/ | TA-TU / DA-DU |
| Velar | **k** /k/ | **g** /g/ | — | KA-KU (merged) |
| Uvular | — | — | **q** /q/ | QA-QE |

**The begadkephat rule does NOT apply.** In post-biblical Hebrew, six stops (בגדכפת) spirantize after vowels: p → f, t → θ, k → x, b → v, d → ð, g → ɣ. This innovation postdates 1st-millennium BCE and has no place in Bronze Age Minoan. All stops are always stops in every environment.

Linear A merges voicing: P-signs cover both /p/ and /b/; K-signs cover /k/, /g/, and /q/. Pronunciation is restored from etymology. *KU-PA-NU* = /gupānu/ (not /kupānu/) because the Ugaritic cognate is *gpn*.

Gordon §138–146: SA-signs cover /s/, /ʃ/, and possibly /ṯ/. The script neutralizes sibilant distinctions that the spoken language may have preserved.

### The Interdental Question (ṯ, ḏ)

Proto-Semitic had three interdentals: *ṯ (voiceless), *ḏ (voiced), *ẓ̣ (emphatic). Every daughter language merged them—but differently:

| Language | ṯ → | ḏ → | Example |
|----------|-----|-----|---------|
| Ugaritic | ṯ (preserved) | ḏ (preserved) | ʾaṯiratu, ḥadaṯu |
| Phoenician/Hebrew | sh (שׁ) | z (ז) | ʾAshērah, ḥadash |
| Aramaic | t | d | ḥadat |

This is a load-bearing decision. It determines whether we write **Aṯiratu** (archaic, pre-merger), **Ashiratu** (Canaanite merger), or **Atiratu** (Aramaic merger). The same law governs every word with these radicals: ḥadaṯu/ḥadashu/ḥadattu, ṯalāṯu/shalāshu/talāttu ("three"), ṯūru/shōru/tūru ("bull").

**Current position**: Minoan may predate the merger entirely. As an archaic NWS language at the root of the family, it could preserve the interdentals as distinct phonemes (a 24-phoneme inventory). This is the strongest philological argument—Minoan sits before the split, not after it. When composing, prefer the archaic ṯ/ḏ forms and note the Canaanite and Aramaic reflexes.

The skill currently writes **Atiratu** (following Aramaic-path convention from Gordon's transliterations). The archaic form **Aṯiratu** is equally valid and arguably more precise.

| Phoneme | IPA | Latin | Linear A | Notes |
|---------|-----|-------|----------|-------|
| r | /r/ | r | RA-RU | Alveolar trill (not approximant) |
| l | /l/ | l | RA2 | Distinct sign (AB76); rare but attested |

| n | /n/ | n | NA-NU | Assimilates before consonants |

The L/R distinction exists in the syllabary (RA vs. RA2) but is underused. Many words that etymologically have /l/ are written with RA-signs. *KI-RO* = /kull/ ("all/total") uses RA, not RA2.

### Laryngeals and Pharyngeals

| Phoneme | IPA | Latin | Linear A | Notes |
|---------|-----|-------|----------|-------|
| ʾ (aleph) | /ʔ/ | ʾ | A-series (initial) | Glottal stop; implied by initial-A words |
| h | /h/ | h | — | Not in syllabary; implied by roots |
| ḥ (het) | /ħ/ | ḥ | — | Pharyngeal; implied by roots |
| ʿ (ayin) | /ʕ/ | ʿ | — | Pharyngeal; surfaces as /o/ |

These four phonemes are real in the spoken language but invisible in Linear A. The syllabary has no way to write them directly. Their presence is inferred from:
- Etymology: words with known Semitic roots containing these consonants
- Vowel coloring: the ʿayin-to-O shift (below)
- Comparative evidence from Ugaritic and Phoenician

## Vowel System

### Primary Vowels

| Quality | IPA | Latin | Linear A | Notes |
|---------|-----|-------|----------|-------|
| a | /a/ | a | A, -A series | Open; most common |
| i | /i/ | i | I, -I series | Close front |
| u | /u/ | u | U, -U series | Close back |

### Secondary Vowels

| Quality | IPA | Latin | Linear A | Notes |
|---------|-----|-------|----------|-------|
| e | /e/ | e | E, -E series | Less common; may be allophonic |
| o | /o/ | o | O, -O series | Rare in Linear A; see ʿAyin Shift |

The scarcity of O-vowel signs in Linear A (compared to Linear B) supports the Semitic hypothesis: NWS languages have fewer /o/ phonemes than Greek. Most Minoan /o/ arises from the ʿayin shift.

### The ʿAyin-to-O Shift (Gordon §150)

> "The ʿ (cayin) at least in certain combinations, shifts to *o*. Thus *ʿa > o*: *baʿl- > bōl-* (written PU-RA) 'Baal.'"

This is a distinctly Canaanite/Phoenician phonological process:
- Proto-Semitic *baʿlu* → Minoan *bōlu* (written PU-RA)
- Proto-Semitic *naʿlu* → Minoan *nōlu* "sandal"

The shift occurs when ʿayin is adjacent to /a/. The pharyngeal constriction colors the vowel to /o/ as the consonant weakens.

## Syllable Structure

### Underlying
CVC, CVCC (consonant clusters in coda position). Standard Semitic syllable canon.

### Surface (in Linear A script)
CV only. The syllabary forces open syllables:
- /kull/ → KI-RO (two CV signs for a CVC word)
- /yattan/ → JA-TA-NO (three CV signs for CVCCVC)
- /qiryatu/ → KI-RE-JA-TU (four CV signs for CVCCVCV)

Coda consonants are written as part of the next syllable or absorbed. Final consonants may be written with a "dead vowel" (the vowel of the final sign is not pronounced).

## Stress

Penultimate stress (Ugaritic model):
- *ya-TA-nu* "he gives"
- *Ki-re-YA-tu* "city"
- *A-ti-RA-tu* "Athirat"
- *ku-NI-su* "emmer wheat"

Exception: monosyllables and particles carry their own stress: *tun!* "give!", *ki* "so that."

## Phonological Processes

### N-Assimilation
/n/ before a consonant assimilates completely:
- *yintan* → *yittan* "he will give" (n → t before t)
- *ʾanpanu* → *ʾappanu* "face" (n → p before p)

### Voicing Assimilation
In consonant clusters, voicing of the second consonant may spread leftward. Evidence limited; inferred from NWS parallels.

### Gemination
Not marked in Linear A. Reconstructed from etymology:
- *yittan* (geminate /tt/ from n-assimilation of *yintan*)
- *kunāshu* (Akkadian shows geminate /nn/)

## Transcription Conventions

### Latin Transliteration (Primary)
Lowercase, hyphens between morphemes where relevant. Long vowels marked with macron (ā, ī, ū) when etymologically certain. Emphatics: ṭ, ṣ, ḥ, ʿ. Shin: sh.

Example: *yatanu Kupānu yēna la-Atiratu*

### Linear A Syllabograms (Secondary)
UPPERCASE, hyphens between signs. Follow GORILA conventions. J for /y/.

Example: *JA-TA-NU KU-PA-NU JA-NE A-TI-RA-TU*

### Consonantal Skeleton (Scholarly)
Root consonants only, hyphens between. Used for cognate comparison.

Example: *y-t-n g-p-n y-n ʾ-t-r-t*
