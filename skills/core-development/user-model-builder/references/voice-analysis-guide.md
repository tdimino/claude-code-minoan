# Voice Analysis Guide

Open Souls–informed methodology for analyzing a person's voice from their published writing. The goal is to produce a model precise enough to calibrate a soul engine persona or to write convincingly in the subject's voice.

## Prerequisites

Read the entire archived corpus before beginning analysis. Voice analysis from partial reads produces distorted register maps. Load all files from `archive/{platform}/` and read them chronologically.

## Step 1: Register Identification

A register is a distinct mode of expression that a person shifts into based on context. Most people have 2-5 registers.

**Process:**
1. Read through the corpus noting moments where the voice shifts noticeably
2. Name each register with a descriptive label (e.g., "immersive narrator", "social critic", "technical explainer")
3. Estimate frequency as a percentage of total output
4. Identify what triggers each register (topic, audience, emotional state)
5. List 3-5 specific markers (vocabulary, sentence structure, pronoun usage, tense)

**Output format:**

| Register | Frequency | Trigger | Markers |
|----------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Immersive narrator | 50% | Scene-setting, meals, travel | Present-tense, sensory detail, named restaurants, interior monologue |
| Reflective essayist | 30% | Career, health, class | First-person confession, bold thesis statements, dream imagery |
| Social critic | 20% | Inequality, corporate culture | Ironic juxtaposition, rhetorical questions, class-conscious observation |

## Step 2: Tonal Range

Map the emotional spectrum the subject covers. This is distinct from registers—a single register can span multiple tones.

Common tones: earnest, campy/self-aware, elegiac, righteously angry, warmly observational, wry, confessional, instructional, academic, playful.

Note which tones are dominant and which are rare. Rare tones often signal the most characteristic moments.

## Step 3: Sentence Mechanics

Identify the subject's signature patterns at the sentence level:

- **Emphasis devices**: Bold for thesis marking? Italics for internal voice? ALL CAPS (rare)?
- **Punctuation habits**: Em dash frequency and usage (mid-sentence pivots? interruptions?), parenthetical asides (humor? context?), semicolons, ellipses
- **Structural devices**: Section headers as temporal markers? Numbered lists? Block quotes?
- **Pronoun patterns**: First-person dominant? Second-person address? Shifting between?
- **Sentence length**: Short declarative? Long flowing? Alternating?

## Step 4: Characteristic Phrases

Identify recurring verbal patterns unique to the subject:

- **Self-identifying phrases**: "the queen of club sodas with lime", "modern-day mystic"
- **Habitual constructions**: "I feel for [person]", "Wild nights!", "I guess X"
- **Verbal tics**: Opening sentences with "And", trailing thoughts with "—"
- **Cultural reference patterns**: High-low spanning without apology, specific naming of restaurants/places

## Step 5: Worldview Markers

Extract 5-10 recurring themes that appear across multiple posts. These are not topics but underlying concerns that shape how the subject sees the world.

**Process:**
1. After reading the full corpus, list every theme that appears in 2+ posts
2. Group related themes (e.g., "landlord complaints" + "housing instability" + "Engels reference" = "Class Consciousness")
3. Name each theme with a descriptive label
4. Provide 2-3 sentences of evidence with specific post references
5. Note how themes interact (e.g., medievalism as antidote to class struggle)

## Step 6: Calibration Quotes

Select 10-15 quotes that capture the voice at its most characteristic. These are the highest-authority voice samples—they define the soul.

**Selection criteria:**
- At least one quote per identified register
- Cover the full tonal range
- Prioritize quotes that couldn't be written by anyone else
- Include both short punchy quotes and longer flowing ones
- Represent the subject's worldview (not just their style)

**Verification protocol:**
1. Copy the quote directly from the archived source file
2. Verify character-by-character: opening words, punctuation, emphasis, quote marks
3. Do not "clean up" or normalize the quote (preserve original formatting)
4. Do not drop opening conjunctions ("But", "And", "So") even if the quote reads better without them
5. Use the exact quote mark style from the source (single vs double)

## Step 7: Voice Evolution (When Applicable)

When the corpus spans a significant time period (3+ years) or crosses a major life transition (education, career change, health event), compare voice across periods.

**Use a dimension comparison table:**

| Dimension | Period A | Period B |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Register | Primary mode | Primary mode |
| Tone | Dominant tone | Dominant tone |
| Vocabulary | Characteristic word choices | Characteristic word choices |
| Structure | Organizational patterns | Organizational patterns |
| Self-disclosure | Level of personal revelation | Level of personal revelation |
| Imagery source | Where metaphors come from | Where metaphors come from |
| Academic reference | How scholarship appears | How scholarship appears |
| Humor | Presence and type | Presence and type |

**Summarize the evolution in 2-3 key shifts:**
> "From guide to narrator. From elevated diction to controlled colloquialism. From nature metaphor to body/class specificity."

Note what remained constant across both periods—this reveals the deepest layer of voice.
