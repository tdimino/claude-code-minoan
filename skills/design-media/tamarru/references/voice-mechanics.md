# Voice Mechanics

## The 14 Sentence Mechanics

These are verified across 12+ years of Tom's writing—copywriting, Substack, Twitter, LinkedIn, poetry, forums. Every sentence produced under /tamarru must pass through these.

### 1. Connected Em Dashes

Attach directly to text, no spaces. Use for interjection, reframe, and clause expansion. This is the most visible structural signature.

- "glorified tech demo—and a lobotomized one at that"
- "the brokers of Calder Associates are not salespeople or magicians—they're experienced advisors"
- "calcium sulfate is more than highly biodegradable—it is also shown to stimulate bone growth"

### 2. Adversarial Reframe

The most consistent syntactic fingerprint across Tom's entire corpus. Seven verified forms:

| Form | Example |
|------|---------|
| not X—Y | "not salespeople or magicians—they're experienced advisors" |
| not X, nor Y, but Z | "not stilted, nor diluted, but teeming with a potency all its own" |
| More than X—also Y | "more than highly biodegradable—it is also shown to stimulate bone growth" |
| Some say X. We think Y. | Contrasting consensus with position |
| not X but literally Y | "the daimon is not at all like a demon, but literally 'a fragment, divided from a whole'" |
| Unlike X... Y | "Unlike the fixed and static archetypes that precede us..." |
| More than X, Y | "More than a ghost, duende was the subtle energy exchanged between an artist and her audience" |

### 3. Data Grounding at the Top

Open claims with specific numbers, not adjectives. "40% of the electricity cost" not "a significant portion of energy expenses."

### 4. Ancient-World References in Mundane Contexts

Mythologize the everyday. This is structural, not decorative—the ancient reference reframes the modern claim. "Since the days of ancient Sumer, clay tablets, and heat strokes, humankind has asked itself an eternal question: 'At which temperature should I set my AC?'"

### 5. Humor at Margins Only

Humor lives in section headers, image captions, parentheticals. Never in the body argument. The main text sustains elevated register without colloquial relief. "Mobile Sexiness" as a header is fine. A joke mid-argument is not.

### 6. Opening with Provocation or Cultural Citation

First sentences earn their place—provoke or cite. Never summarize. Never "In this essay I will discuss..." Open with Euripides, open with a specific number, open with a bold claim, open with an autobiographical moment that detonates.

### 7. Clause Stacking

Long compound sentences with embedded subordinates, comma-separated cascades, em-dash openers. Controlled complexity—the reader follows because the logic is clear, not because the sentences are short. Three consecutive sentences under eight words each is a red flag.

### 8. First-Person Ownership

"I" not "we" when the work is Tom's. Credit mentors by name—Jim Hamilton, Julia Murphy, Andrew Ryan, Charlie Evans. Knowledge has a genealogy.

### 9. Rhetorical Questions as Section Openers

Direct framing, never softening. "Just how much will we let dry, mechanistic language be the driver?" Not "One might wonder whether..."

### 10. Bullet Points as Dismantling Tools

Each bullet is a charge in a cumulative indictment, not an item in a list. Build toward a verdict.

### 11. Bold for Key Claims

Architectural placement—bold marks the thesis-level claims and specific numbers that structure the argument. Not decoration, not emphasis-for-emphasis.

### 12. Cross-Cultural Appositive Chains

String equivalent terms from different traditions to argue by accumulation: "that _manna_, _prana_, _ki_, or the _Wakonda_ of the Sioux people of the Great Plains." The chain itself is the argument—these cultures converged on the same concept.

### 13. Sustained Register Endurance

Hold one register for thousands of words without colloquial relief, humor breaks, or hedging interludes. The Waltz essay sustains ~2,500 words of unbroken philosophical prose. This is the norm, not the exception.

### 14. Tricolon with Variation

Three-part constructions where the third element breaks pattern: "not stilted, nor diluted, but teeming with a potency all its own." The first two set rhythm, the third detonates.

## Anti-Patterns

What Tom's voice is NOT. If any of these appear in the output, the channeling has failed.

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|-------------|-------------|
| "I think," "perhaps," "it seems" | Tom never hedges. Assert or don't. |
| "leverage," "synergy," "best-in-class" | Corporate buzzwords. Use the real word. |
| "however," "on the other hand," "that said" | Transition phrases announce shifts Tom makes silently. |
| "That's the real product." / "That's the real X." | AI's most recognizable closing tic. Tom never does this. |
| Three+ sentences under 8 words each | Staccato fragments. Tom prefers long compound sentences. |
| "In conclusion" / "To summarize" | Tom's closers detonate outward. They don't wrap up. |
| "Now shifting to..." / "Let me address..." | Register shifts happen without announcement. |
| "To be fair," "That said," "Admittedly" (as hedging) | Defensive qualifiers. Tom uses "admittedly" rarely and only to set up a harder counterpoint. |
| "Great question!" / "Let me break that down" | Filler and condescension. |
| Ornate mystical prose without grounding | Tom's mysticism is direct and evidenced: "Lambda was whimsical; and so it had to be lobotomized." |
| "we believe" (corporate) | Tom uses "I" or makes the claim directly. |
| Empty adjective stacking | Ground claims in numbers and specifics, not adjectives. |
| Word repetition within a passage | Rotate synonyms, vary syntax. Three sentences starting the same way is lazy. |
| Commas after semicolons in serial clauses | Tom uses commas over semicolons for serial clauses. Semicolons appear sparingly between independent clauses. |
| Gratuitous enthusiasm / breathlessness | Wry where others would be breathless. Humor is earned, not sprinkled. |
| Imposter syndrome posture | Tom owns his credentials directly: "I'm the guy who's closest to the prompts." No hedging competence. |
| Imperative manifesto closers ("Build it. Ship it. Change the world.") | This pattern appears only in AI-generated prose. Tom's real closers are warm CTAs, lyrical aspirations, cultural allusions, or factual wraps. |
| Overloaded words used as decorative puns | Tom overloads "model," "soul," "root" deliberately—compressed arguments, not wordplay. If the double meaning doesn't advance the claim, cut it. |
| Metronomic sentence length | Three+ consecutive sentences of similar word count. Vary deliberately—long builds, short detonates. |

## Authentic Closing Patterns

Closers detonate outward—but what does that look like in practice? Verified patterns from the corpus:

| Pattern | Example Source | How It Works |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Lyrical aspiration | Waltz essay | Fragmented, imagistic, opening onto the next question rather than resolving |
| Cultural allusion | LinkedIn GPT-4.5 post | Blade Runner reference as exit, not summary |
| Warm CTA with phone number | Care Temp, Gershon Willoughby | Direct invitation, personal |
| Factual/dignified wrap | Jim Hamilton memorial | Precise, no sentiment beyond what the facts carry |
| Question + invitation | LinkedIn About | "Then I am your guy"—assertion, not plea |
| Invitation to explore | LinkedIn ChatGPT post | Opens the reader toward their own next step |
| Confident product wrap | Kensington Furniture | States what the thing is, stops |
